\section{Algorithms for Dense and Sparse Matrix Multiplication}
\label{sec:bg:algos}

\lettrine{T}{he} main matrix multiplication kernels that are of interest are \acrfull{mv} and \acrfull{mm}, which include \acrshort{spmv} and \acrshort{spmspv} ($A \times b = c$), \acrshort{spgemv} ($A \times b + d = c$), \acrshort{spmm} and \acrshort{spmspm} ($A \times B = C$), and \acrshort{spgemm} ($A \times B + D = C$), with `Sp' for \emph{sparse} and `GE' for \emph{general}.
\acrshort{spmv}, as well as its variant \acrshort{spgemv}, are by far the dominant operations in modern algebraic kernels (linear solvers, eigensolvers) which have been explicitly based on them~\cite{Gilbert2008, Duff2002_SparseBLAS, Penn2006, Yamazaki2011, Itoh1995, Kjolstad2017_TACO, Hapla2013}.
Since these kernels are ubiquitous, their efficient implementation is primordial.
In contrast, the \acrshort{spmspm} multiplication is less frequent in standard algebraic kernels, but it is extensively used in algebraic graph manipulation~\cite{Dongen2000, Kepner2015, Gilbert2007, Rabin1989, Chan2007, Azad2015, Brin1998}, as well as in multigrid solvers~\cite{Brandt2003, Li2021, Ryabkov2022, Ayyar2023}.
The growing importance of large graphs has motivated new research on optimizing \acrshort{spmspm} applications.

In the following sections, we focus on \acrshort{spmv} and \acrshort{spmspm}, with the notation $A(M,K) \times b(K) = c(M)$ and  $A(M,K) \times B(K,N) = C(M,N)$, respectively.
The \gls{rank} $K$ is the \gls{crank} between the two inputs $A$ and $B$ (or $b$).
The existence of different \glspl{rank} allows several algorithmic possibilities to compute matrix multiplication: the \acrfull{ip} algorithm, the \acrfull{op} algorithm and \acrlong{gust}'s (\acrshort{gust}) algorithm~\cite{Faddeev1981_InnerOuter, Gustavson1978_GustAlgo}.
In all cases, the matrix $A$ is traversed sequentially.
The three algorithms, however, impose different access patterns for $B$ (or $b$) and $C$ (or $c$).
Indeed, in many cases, they have to be accessed multiple times, as discussed in the following sections.

\begin{highlight}{Note}{SkyBlue}
    In this manuscript, we focus on only two kernels, \acrshort{spmv} and \acrshort{spmspm}.
    Note that, among \acrshort{mv} kernels, \acrshort{spmv} is the most used and we specifically work on this kernel from Chapter~\ref{cha:spdc}.
    \acrshort{spmspm} is also widely used and studying this \acrshort{mm} kernel is of interest as the memory access patterns in both matrices are irregular.

    The \emph{general} (`GE') version of these kernels is not fundamentally different, as the majority of the compute operations are in the matrix multiplication, so going forward, we will ignore the `GE' variants.
\end{highlight}

\subsubsection{Inner-Product Algorithm}

The \acrlong{ip} algorithm~\cite{Faddeev1981_InnerOuter} is an intuitive way to calculate a matrix multiplication because it follows the loop order of the mathematical formulas~\eqref{eq:bg:MVinner} and~\eqref{eq:bg:MMinner} for \acrshort{mv} and \acrshort{mm}, respectively.
The \gls{crank} $K$ is the innermost loop of the algorithm (see Algorithms~\ref{alg:bg:MVinner} and~\ref{alg:bg:MMinner}, lines 2 and 3, respectively).
The output $C$ (or $c$) is always updated sequentially, but the $B$-matrix (or $b$-vector) will be read entirely $M$ times.
In the context of sparse matrices, the reads of $B$ (or $b$) are conditioned by the non-zero elements of $A$, which requires indirect accesses.
If $B$ (or $b$) is also sparse, the algorithm needs to perform \glspl{intersection} between the non-zero elements in the rows of $A$ and the columns of $B$ (or in the $b$-vector) (see Section~\ref{sec:bg:hwchallenges}).
In Algorithm~\ref{alg:bg:MMinner}, exchanging $M$ and $N$ (lines 1 and 2) simply causes the output to be updated \emph{column-wise}.

\begin{equation}
    \forall i \in \ldb0, M-1\rdb, \; c_{i} = \sum_{k = 0}^{K-1} A_{i,k} \times b_{k}
    \label{eq:bg:MVinner}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
    \forall (i, j) \in \ldb0, M-1\rdb \times \ldb0, N-1\rdb, \; C_{i, j} = \sum_{k = 0}^{K-1} A_{i,k} \times B_{k,j}
    \label{eq:bg:MMinner}
\end{equation}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \begin{minipage}[t]{0.49\linewidth}
        \begin{algorithm}[H]
        \caption{Dense \acrlong{mv} \acrlong{ip} Algorithm}
        \label{alg:bg:MVinner}
        \For(\tcp*[f]{row first}){$i \in \ldb 0, M-1 \rdb$}{
            \For{$k \in \ldb 0, K-1 \rdb$}{
                $c_{i} \acc A_{i,k} \times b_{k}$
            }
        }
        \end{algorithm}
    \end{minipage}
    \hfill
    \begin{minipage}[t]{0.49\linewidth}
        \begin{algorithm}[H]
        \caption{Dense \acrlong{mm} \acrlong{ip} Algorithm}
        \label{alg:bg:MMinner}
        \For(\tcp*[f]{A-row first}){$i \in \ldb 0, M-1 \rdb$}{
            \For{$j \in \ldb 0, N-1 \rdb$}{
                \For{$k \in \ldb 0, K-1 \rdb$}{
                    $C_{i,j} \acc A_{i,k} \times B_{k,j}$
                }
            }
        }
        \end{algorithm}
    \end{minipage}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Outer-Product Algorithm}

The \acrlong{op} algorithm~\cite{Faddeev1981_InnerOuter} has the opposite \gls{rank} loop order than the \acrlong{ip}.
Indeed, Algorithms~\ref{alg:bg:MVouter} and~\ref{alg:bg:MMouter} show that the \gls{crank} $K$ is the outermost loop (line 1).
It changes nothing mathematically except that the $B$-matrix (or $b$-vector) is always read sequentially.
In the context of sparse matrices, the output $C$ (or $c$) is updated with indirect accesses.
The algorithm needs to perform \glspl{reduction} of the \glspl{psum} $A_{j,k} \times B_{k,j}$ (or $A_{j,k} \times b_{k}$), as we show in Section~\ref{sec:bg:hwchallenges}.
To avoid irregularity when updating $C$ (or $c$), the \acrshort{op} algorithm is often separated into two phases.
First, during the \emph{multiply} phase, we compute several \acrfullpl{po} consisting of intermediate matrices (or vectors) of \glspl{psum} $A_{j,k} \times B_{k,j}$ (or $A_{j,k} \times b_{k}$), then during the \emph{merge} phase, we accumulate all the \acrshortpl{po} to form the final $C$-matrix (or $c$-vector).
With this method, \acrshort{op} necessitates the generation of at most $K$ \acrshortpl{po} of densities conditioned by the non-zeros elements of both inputs $A$ and $B$ (or $b$).

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \begin{minipage}[t]{0.49\linewidth}
        \begin{algorithm}[H]
        \caption{Dense \acrlong{mv} \acrlong{op} Algorithm}
        \label{alg:bg:MVouter}
        \For(\tcp*[f]{column first}){$k \in \ldb 0, K-1 \rdb$}{
            \For{$i \in \ldb 0, M-1 \rdb$}{
                $c_{i} \acc A_{i,k} \times b_{k}$
            }
        }
        \end{algorithm}
    \end{minipage}
    \hfill
    \begin{minipage}[t]{0.49\linewidth}
        \begin{algorithm}[H]
        \caption{Dense \acrlong{mm} \acrlong{op} Algorithm}
        \label{alg:bg:MMouter}
        \For(\tcp*[f]{common rank first}){$k \in \ldb 0, K-1 \rdb$}{
            \For{$i \in \ldb 0, M-1 \rdb$}{
                \For{$j \in \ldb 0, N-1 \rdb$}{
                    $C_{i,j} \acc A_{i,k} \times B_{k,j}$
                }
            }
        }
        \end{algorithm}
    \end{minipage}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Gustavson's Algorithm}

As the \acrshort{mm} algorithms have three \glspl{rank} instead of two for \acrshort{mv}, there is a third loop ordering, known as \acrlong{gust}'s algorithm~\cite{Gustavson1978_GustAlgo} where the \gls{crank} $K$ is the middle-loop (see line 2 in Algorithm~\ref{alg:bg:MMgustavson}).
With this approach, the output is computed row-by-row with \acrshort{po}-rows generated and accumulated for each output $C$-row.
With this algorithm, updates of the $C$-rows are sequential, but elements within the rows are updated with indirect accesses.
In the dense case, the $B$-matrix is entirely read $M$ times and $K$ \acrshort{po}-rows, of size $N$, are generated.
In the context of sparse matrices, conversely to the $C$-rows, the selection of the rows in $B$ is indirect, but the accesses are sequential within a row.
Thus, the \gls{intersection} search is only needed for the $B$-rows and not each element within rows.
The \acrshort{po}-rows can be accumulated before computing the next $C$-row.
Overall, this algorithm can be seen as a trade-off between \acrshort{ip} and \acrshort{op} for \acrshort{mm} kernels, since the indirect accesses are shared between $B$ and $C$.
Again, for this algorithm, exchanging $M$ and $N$ causes the three matrices to be accessed \emph{column-wise} instead of \emph{row-wise}.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \begin{minipage}[t]{0.49\linewidth}
        \begin{algorithm}[H]
        \caption{Dense \acrlong{mm} \acrlong{gust}'s Algorithm}
        \label{alg:bg:MMgustavson}
        \For{$i \in \ldb 0, M-1 \rdb$}{
            \For(\tcp*[f]{common rank}){$k \in \ldb 0, K-1 \rdb$}{
                \For{$j \in \ldb 0, N-1 \rdb$}{
                    $C_{i,j} \acc A_{i,k} \times B_{k,j}$
                }
            }
        }
        \end{algorithm}
    \end{minipage}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Comparison of the Algorithms}

In Table~\ref{tab:AlgosCompSum}, we summarize the main features of \acrshort{ip}, \acrshort{op} and \acrshort{gust} in terms of accesses to the matrix $A$, $B$ (or $b$) and $C$ (or $c$).
From this \gls{highLevel} analysis, we can easily see that the data that is accessed with indirections shifts depending on the algorithm, from the input $B$ (or $b$) to the output $C$ (or $c$), with \acrshort{gust} being a trade-off between \acrshort{ip} and \acrshort{op} for \acrshort{mm} multiplication.
To have a better understanding of the consequences on the actual number of memory accesses, we propose a more detailed analysis in Section~\ref{sec:bg:AnalyticalModel}, for the case of \acrshort{spmspm}.

\begin{highlight}{Note}{SkyBlue}
    Note that these algorithms are available in dense as well as in sparse \acrshort{mv} and \acrshort{mm} multiplications.
    In the sparse variants, the above algorithms (from \ref{alg:bg:MVinner} to \ref{alg:bg:MMgustavson}) have the same behavior from a mathematical point-of-view, but need adjustments to traverse a \gls{spFormat} instead of a dense matrix.
    Sparse versions of these algorithms are presented in Section~\ref{sec:bg:hwchallenges}.
\end{highlight}

\begin{highlight}{Conclusion}{Red}
    There are \textit{three main algorithms} to compute sparse \acrshort{mv} and \acrshort{mm} kernels, \textbf{\acrfull{ip}}, \textbf{\acrfull{op}} and \textbf{\acrfull{gust}}.
    Each presents different characteristics, the main one being the \textbf{irregularity of the accesses}: on the \textbf{input} matrix $B$ (or vector $b$) with \acrshort{ip}; on the \textbf{output} matrix $C$ (or vector $c$) with \acrshort{op}; on both with \acrshort{gust} but at \textbf{row scale}.
\end{highlight}

\subsection{Tiling}
\label{sec:bg:tiling}

The \gls{tiling} process~\cite{Grunbaum1987_Tiling} consists in partitioning a matrix: a matrix may be divided into \glspl{tile}, non-overlapping sub-matrices.
In other words, this method consists of adding \glspl{rank} to delimit the \glspl{tile}, and thus adding loop iterations in the kernel algorithm.

\Gls{tiling} methods make it possible to process smaller portions of the matrix and thus to adapt the working set to the size of the local memory~\cite{Lam1991_Tiling, Wilkinson2023_Tiling}, particularly when the matrix has a structure with denser regions.
\Gls{tiling} can also increase parallelism opportunities.
Software optimizations for sparse matrix computation, such as OSKI~\cite{Vuduc2005} and Sparse BLAS~\cite{Duff2002_SparseBLAS}, are based on \gls{tiling}.
In previous sections, the study of the \acrshort{ip}, \acrshort{op} and \acrshort{gust} algorithms highlighted the influence of \gls{rank} ordering on the sequentiality and data movement, and thus, adding intermediate \glspl{rank} with \gls{tiling} breaks the regularity of pure \acrshort{ip} or \acrshort{op} algorithms, requiring additional hardware support (see Section~\ref{sec:bg:hwchallenges}).

For example, let us consider the $A$-matrix in Figure~\ref{fig:bg:tiling_ex} which is divided into four \glspl{tile} (one level of \gls{tiling}).
For a \acrshort{spmv}, two new \glspl{rank} appear for a total of four \glspl{rank}, as shown in Algorithm~\ref{alg:bg:tiling_ex}: $M_1$ and $K_1$ allow \gls{tile} selection and $M_2$ and $K_2$ allow data access within a \gls{tile}.
Thus, we could decide to apply an \acrshort{op} on \glspl{tile} and conversely an \acrshort{ip} within \glspl{tile}, mixing-up the pros and cons of these two algorithms at different \gls{rank} levels.
In the case of the example in Figure~\ref{fig:bg:tiling_ex}, the upper-left $A$-\gls{tile} (\textit{red}) is evaluated first, sequentially updating the upper \gls{tile} of the $c$-vector in an \acrshort{ip} manner.
The lower-left $A$-\gls{tile} (\textit{yellow}) is then computed the same way.
At this point, \glspl{intersection} have been done with only the upper $b$-\gls{tile}, and $c$ has been updated sequentially (first \acrshort{po}).
The two next $A$-\glspl{tile} (\textit{green}, then \textit{blue}) will compute \glspl{intersection} with the lower $b$-\gls{tile} and update the entire $c$-vector sequentially, showing the \acrshort{op} characteristic of having \acrshortpl{po} to \emph{merge}, two in this case.

        \label{alg:bg:tiling_ex}

                    \For{$k_2 \in \ldb 0, K_2-1 \rdb$}{
                        $i \gets i_1 \times M_2 + i_2$\\
                        $k \gets k_1 \times K_2 + k_2$\\

            }
        }

\begin{highlight}{Definition}{LimeGreen}
    \textbf{\Gls{tiling}} is a method for partitioning matrices in non-overlapping sub-matrices, called \textbf{\glspl{tile}}.
\end{highlight}

\begin{highlight}{Note}{SkyBlue}
    Note that in case of \gls{tiling}, the $A$-matrix is no longer read sequentially, which can be problematic if it is stored in a classical \gls{spFormat} like \acrshort{csr} that is specifically made for \emph{row-wise} accesses.
    Custom \glspl{spFormat} adapted for \gls{tiling} can be used \cite{Im2004_TilingBCSR}, but this requires preprocessing to perform the conversion.
\end{highlight}

\begin{highlight}{Conclusion}{Red}
    \Gls{tiling} methods are widely used to \textbf{distribute workloads} across multicore systems, and to \textbf{locally reduce the data dimensions}.
    Complex \gls{tiling} algorithms with many possible \gls{rank} ordering \textbf{inherit the behaviors of \acrshort{ip} and \acrshort{op}}, and can be described as a combinations of both at different \gls{tile} levels.
\end{highlight}
